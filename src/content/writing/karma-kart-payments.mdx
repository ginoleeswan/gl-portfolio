## The constraint you don't get to skip
Karma Kart ships from one Expo codebase to iOS, Android, and the web. The moment you add a
paid tier, that single codebase hits a wall that has nothing to do with your code: **Apple and
Google require their own in-app-purchase systems for digital goods sold inside native apps.**
You don't get to bring a Stripe checkout to the App Store. The web has the opposite rule — no
native billing API, no 30% gatekeeper, so you're free to use a normal processor.

So "add subscriptions" was never one problem. It was two, wearing the same UI.

## Why I didn't force one tool to do both
The tempting move is to pick a single billing SDK and stretch it across all three platforms. I
didn't, for two reasons:

- **RevenueCat is excellent at native and absent on web.** It wraps StoreKit and Google Play
  Billing and handles receipt validation, entitlements, and restore-purchases — the genuinely
  painful parts of native IAP. It is not a web checkout. Pretending it is means reimplementing
  the thing it's actually good at.
- **A web processor can't satisfy Apple inside the app.** Routing native purchases through
  Paddle or Stripe is exactly what gets a build rejected.

Fighting the platforms here is a tax with no upside.

## What I actually built
One **subscription concept** — Karma Kart+ — expressed through two rails:

- **Native (iOS / Android):** RevenueCat over StoreKit and Play Billing. It owns entitlement
  state on the device.
- **Web:** Paddle, chosen specifically because it's a **merchant of record** — it handles
  global sales tax and VAT so I don't have to register for tax in every jurisdiction a web
  subscriber might come from.

The rest of the app never asks *which processor paid for this.* It asks one question — *is this
user premium?* — against a single entitlement check in `lib/payments`. The platform split lives
behind that boundary; every feature-gating call above it stays platform-agnostic.

## The trade-off I took on purpose
Two rails means two sources of truth for "is this person subscribed," reconciled to one
entitlement. That's more moving parts than a single Stripe integration on a web-only product. I
accepted it deliberately: it's the price of being installable on the App Store **and** sellable
on the open web from the same codebase — which, for a values-driven shopping app that wants to
be wherever people shop, is the entire point.

The principle I'd carry forward: when a platform hands you a hard constraint, model it as a
**boundary, not a bug.** The split isn't mess — it's the architecture being honest about the
world it ships into.
