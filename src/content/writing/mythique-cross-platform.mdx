## The bet
Mythique is a superhero encyclopedia that needed to feel right on a phone and also work on the
web, without me running two apps. So I built it on Expo's managed workflow: one set of file-based
routes compiled to iOS, Android, and web. Working on my own, keeping a single codebase instead of
two is most of what made shipping to three platforms realistic.

## What it costs
Managed Expo has a catch. You're inside a native-module boundary, so if a feature needs a native
dependency Expo doesn't support, you either find a config-plugin route or eject and take on the
native build setup you were trying to avoid. The trick is to build within what the managed
workflow already gives you, and to treat dropping to custom native as a real decision rather than
a first resort.

For Mythique, everything I needed (gestures, Reanimated transitions, image handling) was available
inside that boundary, so it worked out. On an app with heavier native requirements I'd probably
choose differently, which is the point — it depends on what the app actually needs.

## Two APIs that don't agree
The data comes from SuperheroAPI and ComicVine, and they have different shapes, different gaps,
and different rate limits. Calling them straight from the client on every screen is slow, it
exposes the third-party keys, and it ties the UI's correctness to two services being up at once.

So I put Supabase in front of them. The keys stay server-side, and the client reads from my
backend, which returns one normalized shape. The UI never has to know which source a character
came from, and when a field is missing in one source it just doesn't render rather than throwing.

## What ties it together
Both choices come down to the same habit: keep a layer between the app and the things I don't
control — the platform's native internals in one case, messy third-party data in the other. Each
one trades some flexibility for a smaller surface to keep in my head, which on a solo build across
three platforms is what kept it manageable. The repo has picked up 58 stars since, for whatever
that's worth.
