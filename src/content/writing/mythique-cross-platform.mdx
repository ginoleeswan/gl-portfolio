## The bet
Mythique is a superhero encyclopedia that had to feel native on a phone and still exist on the
web — without me maintaining two apps. The bet was Expo's **managed workflow**: one set of
file-based routes compiled to iOS, Android, and web. Solo, "one surface to design, test, and
fix" isn't a convenience — it's the difference between shipping three platforms and shipping one.

## What that bet costs
Managed Expo isn't free. You live inside a **native-module boundary**: if a feature needs a
native dependency Expo doesn't support, you either find a config-plugin path or eject and inherit
the native build complexity you were avoiding. So the discipline is to design *within* what the
managed workflow gives you, and to treat "drop to custom native" as a real decision with a real
cost — not a reflex.

For Mythique, everything it needed — gestures, Reanimated transitions, image handling — lived
inside that boundary, so the trade paid off. On an app with heavy native requirements the same
bet would be wrong, and that's the point: it's a bet, made on *this* app's actual needs.

## The other boundary: two APIs that disagree
Mythique's data comes from **SuperheroAPI** and **ComicVine** — two sources with different
shapes, different gaps, and different rate limits. The naive approach is to call them straight
from the client on every screen. That's slow, it leaks third-party keys into the app, and it
makes the UI's correctness depend on two services being up and agreeing with each other.

So I put **Supabase in front of them**. The third-party keys stay server-side. Reads hit my own
backend, which serves a single normalized shape, so the UI never knows or cares which source a
character came from. When a field is missing in one source it degrades gracefully — a character
with no recorded weight simply doesn't show weight, instead of breaking the screen.

## The principle underneath both
Both choices are the same instinct applied twice: **put a boundary between your app and the
things you don't control.** The managed workflow is a boundary against platform-specific native
complexity. The Supabase layer is a boundary against flaky, mismatched third-party data. Each
trades some raw flexibility for a smaller, calmer surface to reason about — and for a solo build
shipping to three platforms, a calmer surface is what makes it shippable at all.

The repo has since picked up 58 stars from other developers, which I read as a small signal that
the boundaries were drawn in roughly the right places.
